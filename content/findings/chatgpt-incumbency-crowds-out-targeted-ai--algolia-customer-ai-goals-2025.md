## Finding

The ChatGPT enterprise license has become a **default substitute** for narrow AI products. The behavior is consistent across the sample: a customer evaluates a vendor's AI feature, decides they can replicate "close enough" with ChatGPT, and cancels the evaluation. This happens *even when the dedicated tool measurably outperforms ChatGPT* — sunk-cost license plus demonstrable team time-savings beats quality delta.

The second-order effect: customers are actively trying to **consolidate AI spend**, so a new AI product needs a clear, unique value over what a ChatGPT prompt can do.

## Mechanism (hypothesized)

Three forces compound:

1. **Sunk cost rationality.** The ChatGPT seat is already paid; marginal usage cost is zero.
2. **Time-savings is the only easy ROI.** AI ROI is hard to prove in general; "freed up the brand team" is provable in week one. Once that bar is met, quality differentials become a luxury.
3. **License sprawl fatigue.** Procurement and security review costs make each *additional* AI vendor expensive in non-dollar ways.

The implication for any new AI product is brutal: it needs to do something that ChatGPT structurally can't replicate against the customer's catalog, ranking layer, and analytics — not something that ChatGPT can do *worse* but cheaply enough.

## Counter-evidence to look for

- Watch how this dynamic evolves as ChatGPT enterprise products mature (custom GPTs over the customer's data, native MCP support, file ingestion at scale). The moat ChatGPT can't cross today may move next quarter.
- May not generalize outside enterprise eCommerce — smaller customers or non-retail verticals may not have ChatGPT seats, removing the substitution effect.
- This is interview-reported behavior; verifying with closed-lost data from sales would harden the finding.
